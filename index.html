Why Istanbul

If you are here, you want to know more about Istanbul and why should be the place to go in your semester, holiday or weekend break. Here a few reasons why Istanbul is the place to run to:

Cultural and History

Istanbul is the only city in the world that it is across two continents: Europe and Asia The Bosphorus Strait divides the city, offering stunning views and the chance to experience two cultures in one city.

Besides that, Istanbul has been the capital of three major empires: the Roman, Byzantine, and Ottoman Empires. Important landmarks like the Hagia Sophia, Topkapi Palace, and the Blue Mosque are steeped in history.

Must-Try Dishes

Istanbul has an amazing cuisine, from delicious meat food like kebabs to amazing sweets like bakhlava, Istanbul is the best place to have food on the Spring or Fall. In Istanbul you will find:

  • Kebabs: Turkish kebabs, such as the Adana made with minced lamb or beef, mixed with spices, and grilled on skewers, are a staple in Turkish cuisine. The Iskender kebab is served on a bed of pita bread, topped with a tomato sauce, yogurt, and melted butter, offering a combination of rich and savory flavors.
  • Lahmacun: Lahmacun is a thin flatbread topped with a mixture of minced meat, typically lamb or beef, herbs, and spices like cumin and paprika. It's often served with a squeeze of lemon and fresh parsley, adding a refreshing taste to the savory dish.
  • Balık Ekmek: This is a popular Istanbul street food: grilled mackerel fish, seasoned and served in a sandwich with lettuce, tomatoes, and onions. The sandwich is a simple yet flavorful choice, best enjoyed at the waterfront in Eminönü, where the lively atmosphere enhances the experience.
  • Baklava: Baklava is made from layers of thin phyllo dough filled with crushed pistachios or walnuts. After baking, it’s soaked in a sweet syrup made from sugar, water, and lemon. For an authentic taste, visit places like Karaköy Güllüoğlu, known for its traditional recipe and perfect balance of sweetness and crunch.

Coffee and Tea Culture

If you enjoy a good coffee or hot drinks, Istanbul needs to be in your list of places to visit. You probably gonna be craving for a good coffee on the Spring with the lower temperature, you gonna find:

  • Turkish Coffee: Visit historic cafes like Mandabatmaz for a strong, aromatic brew served with a side of Turkish delight.
  • Çay (Turkish Tea): Sip tea in tulip-shaped glasses at tea gardens such as Çamlıca Hill or by the Bosphorus while enjoying an amazing view.

Insider Tip: Learn how to interpret your coffee grounds for a fun fortune-telling experience!

In Turkey, coffee fortune-telling, or tasseography, is a traditional and fun cultural practice. After finishing a cup of Turkish coffee, the leftover grounds at the bottom of the cup are interpreted to reveal insights about your past, present, or future. This can include symbols, shapes, or patterns formed by the grounds when the cup is inverted onto a saucer.

Street Food Adventures

We told you about the turkish cuisines, but one of the main types of food that they are really good is street food,

  • Simit: Grab a freshly baked sesame-encrusted bread ring from street vendors—perfect for breakfast on the go.
  • Midye Dolma: Try stuffed mussels with a squeeze of lemon, especially in Taksim or Kadıköy.
  • Kokoreç: For the adventurous eater, sample grilled lamb intestines, a true local delicacy.
  • Chestnuts and Corn: Roasted chestnuts and grilled corn are popular snacks, especially in winter.

Going on Spring or Fall break

If you an NYUAD or any University student you understand the value of a semester break, is the moment to relax. And I also want that for you, we want to make sure that you will be well informed about Istanbul on the Spring and on the Fall.

Spring Break in Istanbul (March)

Weather Characteristics

  • Temperature: Daytime temperatures range from 10°C to 15°C (50°F to 59°F), while evenings drop to around 6°C (43°F).
  • Rainfall: March sees moderate rainfall, with a mix of sunny days and occasional showers.
  • Wind: Coastal areas, especially near the Bosphorus, can be windy, adding a chill to the air.

Clothing Recommendations

  • Pack layered clothing, such as sweaters, long-sleeve shirts, and cardigans.
  • A lightweight jacket or trench coat is perfect for cooler days.
  • Comfortable, waterproof shoes are a must for rainy days.
  • Bring an umbrella or raincoat for unexpected showers.

Activities to Enjoy

  • Start exploring the tulip blooms in **Emirgan Park** and other city gardens.
  • Take a scenic cruise along the Bosphorus Strait to enjoy the crisp spring air.
  • Visit historical landmarks like the Hagia Sophia and the Blue Mosque with fewer crowds than in summer.

Tip: Early March is off-peak, so you can find better deals on hotels and flights.

Fall Break in Istanbul (October)

Weather Characteristics

  • Temperature: Daytime temperatures average 16°C to 20°C (61°F to 68°F), with cooler evenings around 12°C (54°F).
  • Rainfall: Rainfall increases slightly, but most days are mild and sunny.
  • Humidity: The humidity is comfortable, making October a pleasant time to explore the city.

Clothing Recommendations

  • Wear light layers, like t-shirts or blouses, with a sweater or light jacket for evenings.
  • Comfortable walking shoes are essential for exploring the city.
  • Pack a compact umbrella, as sudden rain is possible.

Activities to Enjoy

  • Wander through vibrant bazaars like the Grand Bazaar and Spice Bazaar to soak up the autumn atmosphere.
  • Enjoy the golden foliage in parks such as **Gülhane Park** or along the Bosphorus.
  • Savor seasonal Turkish delights, including roasted chestnuts and fresh figs sold by street vendors.

Tip: October is a popular month for cultural events, so check out local festivals and art exhibitions.

Bargain Guide in Istanbul

Bargaining is an integral part of the shopping and dining culture in Turkey, particularly in markets, smaller shops, and some restaurants. While the art of negotiation might seem intimidating at first, understanding the tactics used by salespeople and knowing how to approach bargaining can save you a significant amount of money and ensure you get the best value for your money. Below is a comprehensive guide to help you navigate the bargaining process effectively.

Common Tactics Salespeople Use and How to Counter Them

  • 1. Inflated Initial Prices

    What They Do: Many sellers start with a price that’s significantly higher than the item's real value, especially when dealing with tourists. They assume you may not know the local pricing norms and hope you’ll accept the initial offer.

    How to Avoid It: Always assume the first price is negotiable and avoid agreeing to it outright. Respond with a counteroffer that is at least 40-50% lower than the initial price.

  • 2. Creating a Sense of Urgency

    What They Do: Salespeople may claim that an item is “the last one” or that a deal is only valid for a limited time to pressure you into making a quick decision.

    How to Avoid It: Stay calm and don’t let the urgency sway you. Politely tell them you need time to think or that you’ll come back later. If the deal is legitimate, they’ll still honor it when you return.

  • Key Phrases in Turkish for Bargaining

    • Çok pahalı! (It's too expensive!)
    • Son fiyat ne? (What is your final price?)
    • İndirim yapar mısınız? (Can you give me a discount?)

  • Best Places to Bargain in Turkey

    • Grand Bazaar, Istanbul: Famous for its variety of goods and rich history.
    • Spice Bazaar, Istanbul: Known for spices, teas, and local delicacies.
    • Bazaars in Cappadocia: Great for unique souvenirs like pottery and carpets.
    • Local markets in smaller towns like Gaziantep or Antalya: Often less crowded and better for deals.

    Cultural Tips for Bargaining

    • Always greet the seller before starting negotiations.
    • Don’t show too much interest in the item; it might make bargaining harder.
    • Be polite and friendly. Sellers appreciate respectful behavior and are more likely to give a discount.

  • When Not to Bargain

    Bargaining is not appropriate in high-end stores, supermarkets, or chain restaurants. Save your haggling skills for markets and smaller, independent shops.
